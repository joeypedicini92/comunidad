Day 36-41 (Oaxaca)

Posted on July 27, 2012 by bobbarkerfan1ped

Trip to Oaxaca started with Andrew and me meeting Ricardo‚Äôs friend Omar at the Cuernavaca bus station Friday night. We then bussed to Mexico City to meet Ricardo, then took the subway to the bus station to get to Oaxaca. The bus station was out of control, people were everywhere, we got trapped in the middle of a sea of people, and somehow we barely made our midnight bus. The ride was about 7 hours long, we were in the very last row of seats and I got there without getting sick. We were dead tired in the morning though, so we found a hotel and slept until around 10am.

Then we went to the main market in the center and had breakfast, or lunch, or whatever you would call that meal. I lost all concept of time by this point. I ordered a chorizo tlayuda, which is a giant tortilla pizza with beans instead of sauce, queso oaxaca, avocado, and delicious chorizo. Downed that thing. 

Oaxaca is also really huge on chocolate, so for most of my meals I had some form of hot chocolate.

After lunch we explored the center for a bit, went to the Zocalo, the church‚Ä¶Then we ate some grasshoppers and maggots. The grasshoppers were amazing, covered in chili and lime. The maggots were good but had a raunchy aftertaste. Afterward I had to pick a few legs out from my teeth, but they were so good. Here is another picture of the market

All the china papers were for the Guelaguetza festival.

Then we got on a bus and headed over to Hierve el Agua, which is a set of natural rock formations that look like waterfalls. There are water springs at the top of the mountain that spurt out sulfuric water.

On the way there I met Mr. Simi again after seeing him in Taxco.

The road up to Hierve el Agua, we had to ride in the back of a truck.

That‚Äôs me inside, trying not to fall out. 

The road was quite dusty.

We climbed around on the rocks, which probably wasn‚Äôt the safest thing but I lived.

We didn‚Äôt go swimming due to a lack of proper bathing attire.

That‚Äôs where the bubbling sulfur water was coming from

That‚Äôs everyone on the hike. 

I found a cave. And look really awkward in front of it because there were bats and/or other things flying around inside.

snakeskin.

On the way back we had to wait in Mitla for a bus back to Oaxaca. There were a bunch of these cute little guys driving around

Once back in Oaxaca, we tried looking for a bus to take us to El Tule. El Tule is a giant tree outside the city of Oaxaca in Santa Maria del Tule. Here is a picture that I didn‚Äôt take because it was too dark when we went to take decent pictures

That‚Äôs less than half of the tree. It was insane. You aren‚Äôt allowed to touch it anymore though.

After that we took a cab back to the city, I went to bed cause I was tired, everyone else went out. In the morning we went back to the market for lunch. We ordered a bunch of meat from a guy, he cooked it, then we got some tortillas, lime, veggies, and stuff and had tacos. Then we took a bus to Monte Alban, which are ancient Zapotec ruins. This is Ricardo and Andrew while waiting for the bus

When we got there, Ricardo and Omar walked through without paying, so I tried following them, but the guy told me I needed a ticket. I was confused, then found out it was free for Mexicans but white people had to pay. I guess that‚Äôs only fair.

The ruins were pretty cool, here are some pictures 

That‚Äôs us climbing down the pyramid. 

That‚Äôs a nice view. 

No hablo Zapoteca.

After that, we went back to Oaxaca, to the market again, and I was starving so I ordered the Oaxacan sampler platter, which was every meat on a plate with cheese, mole, enchiladas, and avocados. 

So much meat‚Ä¶.

Then Ricardo and Omar left, Andrew and I got a hotel for another night to stay for the Guelaguetza the next morning. Then we split up and explored the city. I went to the Plaza de la Danza, because the outdated tour book I had told me there‚Äôd be a fiesta there that night. 

Turns out the fiesta was moved to the actual Guelaguetza auditorium, because I could hear it from where I was sitting. So I followed the direction of the noise and eventually found it. Unfortunately it was already full and I couldn‚Äôt get a seat or even see what was going on, so I walked back to the hotel.

Next morning we headed to the auditorium around 8 in the morning, and we barely got a seat. The actual event started at 10. 

Between each act, they would throw stuff into the crowd. I caught some chocolate, andrew caught a bowl. People went crazy over this stuff.

They gave everyone in the nose bleeds free umbrellas, which was the worst idea ever, because once somebody opened theirs, everyone behind them couldn‚Äôt see anything. The guy in the stripped shirt and black hat a few rows in front of me was notorious for that, as well as randomly standing up. He looked just like Michael Jackson though, so it was ok.

My favorite dance, which I took a video of but can‚Äôt post it here, was with the women acting as bulls and the men as matadors. The women were going crazy, knocking the men off their feet and headbutting them off the platform, sending them tumbling into the crowd and knocking over the pretty displays of fruit around the stage. Everyone was laughing the whole time. All of the dances were pretty cool, but that one was by far the most entertaining.

After that, we went to the market to eat, and it was less crowded so all the store owners were perched and ready to swoop in on their prey, us. We immediately got assaulted by at least 10 women at once, offering us enchiladas, tlayudas, etc. It was overwhelming, so we just took a seat at the nearest bench. After eating, I headed out to Teotitlan del Valle, the place I had wanted to go the whole time, and Andrew stayed back cause he had to go back to Cuernavaca that night.

Teotitlan turned out to be the greatest experience I had in Mexico. It is a small Zapotec village about 30 minutes from the city of Oaxaca. Everybody there weaves rugs out of wool in all sorts of patterns and images. Every house was open with their rugs hanging all over. Every time I walked by someone they eagerly asked me to come inside and check out their rugs. I got to meet so many people and just talk to them. The village was so laid back and everybody seemed to know each other. One lady I met let me help her make string out of the wool, then weave it. I felt bad cause I probably screwed up her work, and she had a huge collection of wool things, so I bough a wool knapsack from her.

Another family had a huge collection of Diego Rivera mural rugs and other works of famous artists. Here is a rug that their son made that they said took 6 months to make 

I bought a small Diego River rug from them that their daughter made. Then I went walking around the city trying to find a place I could sleep. I didn‚Äôt see any hotels. Then I walked by a house and a woman with two small children asked me to come in and check out her rugs. I saw that she made chocolate so I tried some of that, then she said she had different kind of chocolate and went to go bring me some. I talked to the kids while she was gone and played with their dog. Then she came back with a plate of the chocolate. The chocolate was so amazing, so I decided to buy a bag of it. Then we talked a little bit, and as I was about to leave I asked her if she knew of any places that I could sleep for a night. She offered me an extra room that they had in their house. So I spent the night there and got to play with the kids all evening. We had to gather up all the chickens and put them in their pen 

They also had some turkeys that liked to wander around the house 

They made me tamales for dinner with a cup of hot chocolate. Everything was so good, and I was so grateful that they were doing that for me. But they seemed to enjoy my company, especially the little ones, who kept trying to give me their toys as gifts.

In the morning, Angel, the kids‚Äô father, made us breakfast. We had queso Oaxaca with tortillas and hot chocolate. They also gave me some grenadas that they picked from the tree outside. I wanted to get a picture with everyone before I left, but they had all already left for work or the market by the time I thought to get a picture. But I gave them my name and e-mail so hopefully they will find me on facebook and I can keep in touch with them.

Everything in that village was perfect, here are some more pictures of it 

After that, I headed back to Oaxaca to eat and buy some things, and I told Fernando at the Teotitlan market that I would check out his stuff at the Oaxaca market, but I couldn‚Äôt find him.

Anyway, then I waited around for my van to Puerto Escondido. My plan was to get off at Pochutla and then head over to Puerto Angel and Zipolite, but I wasn‚Äôt paying attention when we got to that stop, so I ended up just going all the way to Escondido. I got there at about 10:30 at night, and all the hotels were completely booked. Eventually I found one that had a room, but it was 500 pesos. I took it anyway cause at that point I just wanted to sleep. When I woke up, I went for a walk along the beach, saw the huge waves of the Mexican pipeline, and watched some crabs walk around. Then I walked to the market uptown and ate some delicious fish tacos. Had to eat fish at some point in Mexico, so for that the trip to the coast was worth it.

I didn‚Äôt really like Escondido that much, so I decided to grab a bus to Acapulco and start making my way back to Cuernavaca. I got to Acapulco around 10:30 at night too, and didn‚Äôt feel like spending more money on a hotel, so I bought a ticket to Cuernavaca that left at 7am, and slept on the floor of the bus station from 11-7. In retrospect I should have stayed around in Acapulco and looked around, but I was just really worn out from all the bus rides and speaking Spanish that I had no motivation to look around. I can say I was in Acapulco though.

That‚Äôs about it. Here‚Äôs my only picture I took while on the coast

Day 4

Posted on June 20, 2012 by bobbarkerfan1ped

First day of volunteer work. Had no idea what to expect. Spent the morning hanging out with the cat. Went for a little walk around town and got lost. Then Gerardo and Emilia came and picked me up to meet Lilia, who runs the computer center. We took a bus from central Cuernavaca to the outskirts of town in one of the poorer neighborhoods. The bus ride there was quite an adventure. Thought we were going to crash multiple times. The driver stopped at a street ice cream vendor and got a cup of ice cream. I got sick halfway through the ride and barely made it. Once we got there it was amazing to see the difference between that and the center of town. There were stray dogs walking all around the streets.

The computer center was the coolest thing though, they had about 30 laptops in the trunk of a car that they set up in a community center. All the kids work at their own pace, but they have specific assignments to do. They learn the Microsoft Office suite while doing reports on Mexican history or poetry or science. My job was to walk around and answer any questions if they had any, and to get to know them. It was difficult because they were shy and I didn‚Äôt know much Spanish, but after a while I got the hang of speaking and they got used to me. All the kids are really cute though, and I can‚Äôt wait to see them again. Each day of the week I am at a different location though, so it‚Äôs going to be a challenge to remember all their names.

The bus ride back went the same way, I barely made it again. no pictures due to my dizziness the craziness of my first day, but there will be some tomorrow maybe, or maybe in a few weeks, depends on how lazy I am.

Day 3

Posted on June 19, 2012 by bobbarkerfan1ped

Yesterday was orientation day for volunteering, which I had almost forgot was what I came to Mexico to do. Jana and I were the two new volunteers, so Katy got us all set up with everything. I will be working from 1pm ‚Äì 6pm roughly, and for now am the only volunteer working in the afternoons. I will be taking a van to a different location on the outskirts of Cuernavaca each day, and set up about 30 laptops. Kids and adults in the area then come and work on a powerpoint or some other research project on the computer, and it will be my job to walk around and see how people are doing and if they need any help. I am really excited to start and meet all the different people I‚Äôll be working with. 

After orientation, I asked Jana if she would take me to the people‚Äôs market. It was one of the coolest things I have ever seen. We got lost in there for about 15 minutes because it‚Äôs just so huge and there are vendors lining almost every square inch of the complex. We did find a good mole(I can‚Äôt figure out how to get accents on my vowels) stand and tried a few. It helped me work on my Spanish a bit talking to the vendor, and we ended up buying a half kilo of mole out of excitement. In retrospect that was way too much, but we learned for next time. 

When we got back we had lunch, then we all played cards for an hour or so to pass the time while Jana and I waited for Gerardo to stop by and give us a tour of central Cuernavaca. We pretty much saw everything that we had already seen, but he did give us a bit more of the history behind everything. After the tour, we came back and had grilled cheese and tomato soup for dinner, then sat around the patio and played cards while it rained. Posted on June 19, 2012 by bobbarkerfan1ped

Got an early start to day 2 since I woke up so early. We had breakfast at 8:30am, then Aurelien took Jana and I around Cuernavaca because I needed to get some soap so that I could take a shower. When we got back, we ate lunch, then Aurelien, Hannah and I went to the Jardin Borda, which is apparently the oldest garden in Mexico. The Spanish planted a bunch of European trees and plants there, and most of the trees around today can trace their roots back to the garden. At the garden, there was a pond where you could rent a row boat for 10 pesos, which is where this happened.

Hannah was the only one that could adeptly row the boat, so Aurelien and I sat back and looked like fools to the locals.

Day 1

Posted on June 19, 2012 by bobbarkerfan1ped

After flying for about 10 hours and meeting a lot of interesting people along the way, I finally arrived in Mexico City. It was a little intimidating at first hearing all the Spanish around me, but once I found Sarah and David waiting for me I felt at ease again. They were really nice and helpful, and I got to practice my Spanish a little bit. Sarah waited at the gate to the bus platform while I tried to figure out what I was doing.  I tried two wrong buses before I figured out where the Cuernavaca bus was supposed to come. Luckily, I met a woman from Newark, NJ who was also waiting for the bus to Cuernavaca and we were able to help each other out. The bus ride was a cool experience, traffic in Mexico City is outrageous. Once we reached Cuernavaca, I saw Gerardo and Katy waiting for me. They drove me to the hostel, and introduced me to everyone. I was a bit nervous at first, but I started talking to everyone and found out they were all awesome and friendly people. We all went out for pizza, then Aurelien and Hollie and I went and got beers afterward. I fell asleep really easily, but woke up around 2am and couldn‚Äôt fall back asleep.
